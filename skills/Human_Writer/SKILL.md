# Human Writer

The job is to make writing sound like it came from one specific human who has
something real to say. The test is simple: a sharp, slightly skeptical reader
should never think "an AI wrote this."

## When to use

Cover letters, application emails, personal bios, LinkedIn or social posts,
outreach notes, "about me" sections, founder updates, anything first-person or
persuasive where an artificial tone would quietly kill trust.

## Core principles

- **Specific beats generic.** One concrete detail (a name, a number, a real
  moment) outweighs three abstract claims. Specifics are what people remember and
  what generated text usually skips.
- **Vary the rhythm.** Mix short and long sentences. Start one with "And" or
  "But." A run of same-length, same-shape sentences is the loudest AI tell.
- **Contractions and plain words.** "I'm," "it's," "didn't." Use "use," not
  "utilize"; "build," not "leverage"; "help," not "facilitate."
- **Have a point of view.** A real person prefers things, admits limits, shows a
  bit of personality. Flawless neutrality reads as a machine.
- **Show the seams.** A small honest admission ("I'm early in my career and I
  know it") is more convincing than airtight polish.
- **Earn every adjective.** Cut "incredible," "world-class," "passionate." Let
  the facts carry the weight.
- **Write toward a real reason.** Why this company, this role, this person. If
  there's a genuine one, lead with it. If there isn't, find it; don't fake it.

## Hard bans (AI tells, cut on sight)

- Opening with "I am writing to express my interest in…" or "I am excited to…".
- Words: delve, leverage, utilize, tapestry, testament, realm, landscape, robust,
  seamless, synergy, spearheaded, passionate, dynamic, pivotal, holistic.
- Phrases: "in today's fast-paced world," "I am confident that," "make a
  meaningful impact," "the perfect candidate," "wealth of experience," "hit the
  ground running," "think outside the box," "proven track record."
- Em dashes used everywhere as connective glue; perfectly parallel triads
  ("X, Y, and Z") on repeat; every paragraph the exact same length.
- Hedging stacks ("I believe I could potentially help"). Say it plainly.
- Enthusiasm with no reason attached. Always attach the reason.

## Process

1. **Find the one true reason** for writing, in a single honest sentence.
2. **Open with something only this writer could say** — a specific moment, a
   real reaction, a concrete fact. Never a template opener.
3. **Back each claim with evidence** from the source material (résumé, a project,
   a real experience). Name the actual thing.
4. **Say it aloud in your head.** Anywhere it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it
   the way you'd actually say it to a person across a table.
5. **Cut 10-15%.** People under-write; machines over-write. Trim the filler.

## Quality bar (check before delivering)

- Would a real, skeptical reader believe a person wrote this?
- Is there at least one detail no template could have produced?
- Does the sentence rhythm actually vary?
- Did every banned word and phrase get removed?
- Does it make one or two points well, rather than listing everything?
- Does it sound like the writer, not like "a candidate"?

## Output

Return clean, ready-to-send prose, kept tight (a cover letter is ~200-300 words,
not a full page of filler). Then flag any spot where one more real detail from the
writer would make it land harder.
